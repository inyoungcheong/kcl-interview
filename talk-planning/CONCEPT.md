# Fixed-Stage Cinematic Research Talk

## Purpose

This project is a web-based presentation format for faculty job talks and research talks.

It is not a visual essay for solitary reading. It is a speaker-led presentation where the screen acts as a cinematic stage, chapter marker, and visual memory aid.

## Core Format

- Single-page web presentation
- Full-screen scenes, usually 100vh each
- Black default background
- Optional full-screen image or video background
- Minimal text, usually one headline or one short claim
- Arrow-key navigation as the primary presentation mode
- Scroll navigation as a fallback
- Web only, no PDF export required
- No presenter notes

## Design Principles

- The speaker carries the argument.
- The screen should not compete with the speaker's voice.
- One scene should communicate one idea.
- Text should be short enough to read in a glance.
- Background media should create context, evidence, or mood.
- Transitions should feel calm, intentional, and cinematic.
- Avoid decorative UI unless it helps the talk.

## Tone

The visual tone should be serious, elegant, and research-centered.

The talk can use strong cinematic transitions, but it should not feel like a marketing site. It should feel like a live research argument with visual rhythm.

\section{Free Speech as an Obstacle to AI Regulation: Empirical and Doctrinal Grounds}\label{form3-2}
\vspace{1em}



\subsection{\BiLingual{Description de la contribution}{ / }{Description of the contribution}}

In the US, First Amendment doctrine has become the principal obstacle to AI regulation and AI-related litigation.\footnote{\label{f-7}On the regulatory side, California's content moderation transparency mandate (AB 587) was struck down in part on First Amendment grounds in \emph{\href{https://cdn.ca9.uscourts.gov/datastore/opinions/2024/09/04/24-271.pdf}{X Corp.~v.~Bonta}}, 116 F.4th 888 (9th Cir.\ 2024). On the litigation side, AI providers have invoked First Amendment defenses in the first AI-defamation case (\emph{\href{https://www.clearyiptechinsights.com/2025/05/georgia-court-dismisses-defamation-lawsuit-against-openai-over-chatgpt-output/}{Walters v.~OpenAI}}, Ga.\ Super.\ Ct.\ 2025) and in ongoing wrongful-death suits (e.g., \emph{\href{https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/69300919/garcia-v-character-technologies-inc/}{Garcia v.~Character Technologies, Inc.}}, M.D.\ Fla.\ 2024; \emph{\href{https://www.courthousenews.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/raine-vs-openai-et-al-complaint.pdf}{Raine v.~OpenAI}}, S.F.~Super.\ Ct.\ 2025).} Since the 1970s, courts have progressively extended free speech protection from individual speakers to corporations and algorithmic processes, leaving legislatures uncertain whether AI services can be regulated at all. My work argues the a free-speech-maximalist reading exacerbates AI's harmful effects on users through both empirical and doctrinal grounds.

Empirically, our survey of 816 verified researchers~\cite{liao2024llmsresearchtoolslarge} documents AI's double-edged effect on intellectual agency, serving as an equalizer for junior, non-white, and non-native-English researchers while homogenizing distinctive scholarly voice for others. A doctrine that treats every AI output as categorically protected speech cannot track this ambivalence.

Doctrinally, my two sole-authored law review articles build a constructive argument. The first~\cite{cheong2022freedom} analyzes content moderation algorithms and argues that large-impact online intermediaries should bear regulatory obligations parallel to the long-standing justifications for broadcasting regulation. The second~\cite{cheong2025emotional} extends the analysis to generative AI and argues that First Amendment should not bar regulations on AI service providers because AI services serve the public indiscriminately and their primary aims are not promoting viewpoints of their developers.

\section{Comparative US--EU Analysis from Harm Scenarios to Model Audits}\label{form3-3}
\vspace{1em}

\subsection{\BiLingual{Description de la contribution}{ / }{Description of the contribution}}

Traditional legal accountability works after the fact: someone acted with intent, the harm was foreseeable, the affected individuals are identifiable, and the causal chain is traceable. Generative AI breaks all four conditions. Intent is diffused across developers, deployers, and users. Harms are hard to foresee and spread across millions of interactions. Affected individuals are difficult to single out. And causal chains dissolve in opaque model pipelines. My research documents this failure empirically and compares how US and EU law respond.

\emph{Safeguarding Human Values}~\cite{cheong2024safeguarding} adapts threat-modeling methods from computer security research. With a ten-expert interdisciplinary panel, we generated five plausible but unquantifiable harm scenarios spanning educational inequity, insidious fine-tuning bias, chatbot-induced self-harm, and non-consensual virtual abuse. I ran doctrinal analysis against eight domains of US law, including constitutional, civil rights, tort, privacy, and criminal law. The analysis concluded that most US liability doctrines fail to address these harms, a diagnosis subsequently borne out in AI-harm litigation (see \hyperref[f-7]{footnote 7}).

\emph{The Model Hears You}~\cite{he2025deployment} extends this scenario-based approach into runtime model auditing. Testing GPT-4o, Gemini 2.0, and Qwen2-Audio, we documented up to 100\% speaker identification from under-15-second clips of unseen public figures, a refusal rate below 43\% for prohibited workplace emotion detection, and promotion rates twice as high for male voices on identical transcripts. Each behavior plausibly implicates concrete statutes across jurisdictions: US BIPA (Illinois), US Title~VII (federal), EU GDPR, and EU AI Act Article~5(1)(f). These two studies argue that US ex-post liability cannot reach generative AI's diffuse harms, while EU ex-ante prohibition offers a more tractable model.

\subsection{\BiLingual{Originalité et difficulté}{ / }{Originality and difficulty}}

\emph{Safeguarding Human Values}~\cite{cheong2024safeguarding} conducted the novel doctrinal stress-testing of generative AI across eight US legal domains using security-research threat-modeling methods. \emph{The Model Hears You}~\cite{he2025deployment} is, to our knowledge, the first legally grounded runtime audit of audio language models anchored in specific statutory elements. Both studies engage US--EU comparative law analysis, demonstrating that ex-post liability fails. The principal difficulty was cross-doctrinal expansion. Constitutional law, torts, privacy, and criminal law are ordinarily standalone subfields. Trained originally in privacy and free speech, I had to re-specialize across various laws in US and EU to explain why the US cannot easily adopt GDPR-style data protection or general-purpose AI regulation. Over the course of research, reconciling the two disciplinary intuitions required sustained negotiation over what counts as a useful finding.

\subsection{\BiLingual{Validation et impact}{ / }{Validation and impact}}

\emph{Safeguarding Human Values}~\cite{cheong2024safeguarding} appeared in Springer's \emph{AI and Ethics} (2024), and has reached over 15{,}000 accesses and 60+ \href{https://scholar.google.com/citations?view_op=view_citation&hl=en&user=xwZI_jcAAAAJ&citation_for_view=xwZI_jcAAAAJ:hqOjcs7Dif8C}{Google Scholar citations}. Its predictions have tracked subsequent legal developments. For example, Section 230's platform immunity has \href{https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/69300919/garcia-v-character-technologies-inc/}{not extended to generative AI}; product liability has become \href{https://www.nytimes.com/2026/03/25/technology/social-media-trial-verdict.html}{the primary route} for AI-harm plaintiffs, though \href{https://www.courthousenews.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/08/raine-vs-openai-et-al-complaint.pdf}{proving causation remains hard}. \emph{The Model Hears You}~\cite{he2025deployment} was accepted at the 2025 AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics, and Society (AIES). Its empirical demonstration that state-of-the-art audio models circumvent both BIPA's consent requirements and the EU AI Act's workplace emotion prohibition provides the first concrete evidence that current alignment techniques cannot carry the compliance load regulators assume.
