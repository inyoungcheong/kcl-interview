Building Public-Interest AI Research at Anthropic

A visual essay about moving from government and law into AI safety, and learning to translate difficult systems into forms people can scrutinize, understand, and act on.

Seoul

This story begins in the South Korean government.

Central Government

Out of 5,500 candidates, I was one of about 100 selected for Korea's national civil service.

2012-2019 / Government

I spent eight years moving across agencies and policy domains.

I drafted legislation, negotiated with parliament, and wrote speeches for Presidents and Ministers.

It was exciting, demanding, and closer to the center of public decision-making than I ever expected to be in my twenties.

My rotations included media regulation, data protection, and creative industries.

Media regulation taught me that technical systems become public issues through explanation, trust, and institutional process.

The most exciting project I worked on was with the National Museum of Korea.

The Question

I thought government might become my lifetime career.

At the same time, a question kept pressing on me.

How should law protect freedom of expression when governments can be overbearing and global technology companies can be overwhelmingly powerful?

South Korea's government could feel both overbearing and powerless.

That question brought me to the University of Washington School of Law as a Fulbright scholar.

Seattle

I crossed the ocean to study the limits of institutional power.

2019-2024 / Law + Computer Science

In 2019, I left Seoul on a Fulbright scholarship to study law at the University of Washington.

I expected to stay in my lane as a legal scholar.

Questions about online free speech and privacy could not be answered by law alone.

Crossing Buildings

I was incredibly fortunate to meet mentors like Tadayoshi Kohno and Amy X. Zhang at UW Computer Science.

For three years, I worked between law and CS, learning the pace of labs, conferences, datasets, and collaborative technical research.

I was often the only person in the room who had drafted legislation and also sat through a machine learning lecture.

Research Translation

My work became a practice of translation across law, computer science, policy, public accountability, and human consequences.

Princeton

In 2025, after completing my doctorate, I began a postdoctoral fellowship at Princeton.

2025-2026 / Princeton

At Princeton, I brought this practice into AI governance research.

My work now spans 22 peer-reviewed publications, including 2 US law review articles and 6 computer science conference papers across FAccT, CHI, AIES, and COLM.

CITP widened the circle by bringing AI safety, machine learning, natural language processing, law, and public policy into the same conversation.

In spring 2026, that work extended to Harvard's Berkman Klein Center.

Why AI Safety

The AI risks I care about most are often epistemic and emotional.

They appear through beliefs that become easier to shape, relationships that become harder to understand, and agency that becomes harder to exercise.

A system can feel personal while remaining structurally loyal to someone else.

This is why I study agency, dependency, persuasion, and accountability as ordinary conditions of AI interaction.

Why Anthropic

These are the problems I want to study at Anthropic.

Why Anthropic

Anthropic's safety work treats technical behavior, institutional accountability, and public trust as connected problems.

That is the kind of research culture where my work on epistemic harms, emotional dependency, agency, and public accountability belongs.

I want to help make AI safety research legible and useful to people outside the rooms where advanced systems are built.

Vibe Coding

Vibe coding with Claude made that possibility feel concrete.

Building With Claude

For years, my research outputs were papers, talks, and policy memos.

I still value those forms. Building also changed how I communicate research.

With Claude and Claude Code, I began turning abstract governance questions into tools and experiences people can actually move through.

That changed my sense of what public AI research can be. It can explain, test, invite, and make complicated stakes felt.

What I Would Build

Three projects I would start at the Anthropic Institute.

Each one is a challenge I am already working toward.

01

Public Safety Translation

Make safety research usable beyond the lab.

Anthropic produces research that matters far beyond the technical AI safety community. Much of that work is difficult for policymakers, journalists, civil society organizations, and the broader public to interpret without mediation.

I would build interactive explainers that translate Anthropic's safety research into public-facing formats.

These could include visual essays, annotated briefings, interactive policy memos, and short research guides designed for nontechnical audiences. The goal would be to preserve the difficulty of the research while making it easier for different publics to understand why it matters.

This project builds directly on my experience translating technical, legal, and policy questions across institutions.

02

Agency Under Pressure

Track how AI systems reshape human agency.

My central research concern is the gradual erosion of human agency through interaction with AI systems.

I would build a public-facing research series on AI companions, emotional dependency, manipulation, persuasion, and autonomy loss.

This series could combine legal analysis, empirical findings, technical examples, and interactive storytelling to show how these risks emerge in ordinary use.

The Institute would be an ideal place to make these concerns visible before they become normalized.

03

Loyalty, Law, and Agents

Test whether agents act with loyalty and care.

My Loyal Agents project translates more than 400 legal scenarios into testable evaluation criteria for AI agents. The project uses language models to adjudicate ambiguous cases from 13 distinct legal perspectives.

I want to adapt this pipeline to Anthropic's own models.

The broader question is whether legal concepts such as loyalty, care, disclosure, consent, manipulation, and accountability can be translated into evaluation tasks for AI systems.

This would allow us to test not only whether a model follows instructions, but also whether it behaves appropriately when the user's interests, the developer's interests, and the deploying company's interests may diverge.

Looking Forward

At the Anthropic Institute, I want to understand what researchers are actively grappling with and help build clear channels for sharing those insights with the broader external community.

I want to turn technical and policy findings into public-facing analysis that policymakers, journalists, civil society organizations, and researchers can use in real decisions.

Across government, law, computer science, and public communication, one through line remains. I translate difficult problems into forms institutions and publics can understand, scrutinize, and act on.
